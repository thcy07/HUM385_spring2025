What is the difference between augmented reality and virtual reality?

Virtual Reality offers total immersion in a different reality. However, Augmented Reality (AR) shows reality and an altered version. VR replaces what the user sees with an alternate reality, while AR adds to what the user can already see. This means it can be helpful for annotating scenes and providing extra information.
It also puts scenes into context and highlights contrasts with the current reality. VR requires specialist technology, such as headsets, controllers and sensors. AR experiences only need a smartphone or tablet and are downloadable as apps.

Augmented reality has the potential to fundamentally change the way people experience museums. ‚Äù ‚Äî Keir Winesmith & Suse Anderson, "The Digital Future of Museums," Museums and the Web, 2016

What can AR do to change the way people view art?

  • Accessibility: Translate and present content in multiple languages and formats. It is also as cheap as getting a QR code or installing an app. VR can be expensive as it has heavy headsets and difficult to make it accessible to all.
  • Interactivity: Let users zoom, rotate, and learn through play. It interactive enough to keep children in engaged. This also works better for people like me don't know much about art and don't feel much by just looking at art. It can help us find a was to interact with the painting that feel so distant to us.
  • Retention: Studies show AR boosts memory and understanding. This kind of experience is one of a kind and therefore has a high probability to retain in peoples memory. It can also have interactive quizzes that will allows people to remember and learn more about the painting and its history.
  • Inclusivity: Helps people of all ages and backgrounds connect with exhibits. It can also allow people with disabilities also enjoy and interactive experience with these painting. It can allow people to see, listen and read about the paint in 3D.
  • Restoration: I heard that there is a little contention as to wether old sculptures should be restored and if it is who should get the credit. AR can solve this issue where we can keep the original and us AR to restore the sculptures which will still allow artists who restore it to gain recognition and still keep the original.

Conclusion

Augmented Reality is more than just a cool gadget‚Äîit's a tool for deeper education, inclusion, and innovation in museums. As more institutions adopt this technology, we move toward a future where cultural history is not only seen, but felt, explored, and remembered.

My journey through European museums and heritage sites has shown that Augmented Reality is more than just a technological novelty‚Äîit is a powerful tool for transforming how we experience culture, history, and art. While many institutions are still in the early stages of adopting AR, the potential is clear: AR can make exhibitions more interactive, accessible, and memorable for all visitors, regardless of background or ability.

The case studies and on-site impressions reveal both the promise and the current limitations of AR in museums. Some institutions have pioneered immersive experiences that bring extinct animals or lost histories to life, while others are just beginning to explore digital enhancements. Across all examples, AR stands out for its ability to bridge the gap between passive observation and active engagement, offering new ways to learn, explore, and connect with exhibits.

Moving forward, museums can harness AR to create more inclusive and participatory experiences‚Äîmaking art and history accessible to wider audiences, supporting multilingual content, and enabling deeper contextual learning. As technology continues to evolve, so too will the possibilities for storytelling, restoration, and education within cultural spaces. Ultimately, AR invites us not just to see, but to experience and remember the stories that shape our world.
